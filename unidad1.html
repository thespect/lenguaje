UNIT 1: First Language Development

This unit explores the five main theories of L1 acquisition, the developmental stages children go through, and the crucial role of the environment.

1.1 Five Theories of First Language Acquisition (L1)

How do we learn our first language? Here are the main perspectives:

1 Behaviorism (Skinner)
[cite_start]

Central Idea: Language is a learned behavior acquired through imitation, repetition, and reinforcement[cite: 241, 245].

    [cite_start]
  • Learning cycle: Stimulus ‚Üí Response ‚Üí Reinforcement[cite: 246].
    • [cite_start]
  • Example: Child says "water" ‚Üí gets water ‚Üí repeats word[cite: 248].
    • [cite_start]
  • Criticism: Cannot explain creativity (new sentences)[cite: 250].

2 Innatism (Chomsky)
[cite_start]

Central Idea: Humans are born with a "Language Acquisition Device" (LAD) and Universal Grammar[cite: 255, 257].

  • Input only triggers this biological capacity.
    • [cite_start]
  • Explains "poverty of stimulus" (learning with little input)[cite: 259].

  • Example: Children saying "I goed" shows internal rule application[cite: 262].

3 Cognitivism (Piaget)

Central Idea: Language depends on cognitive development. [cite_start]Thinking comes before speaking[cite: 266, 268].

    [cite_start]
  • Children must understand a concept (e.g., time) before using its grammar (past tense)[cite: 270, 273].

4 Interactionism (Bruner)

Central Idea: Language is acquired through social interaction. [cite_start]Caregivers create a LASS (Language Acquisition Support System)[cite: 277, 280].

    [cite_start]
  • Use of "Motherese" (Child-Directed Speech)[cite: 279].
    • [cite_start]
  • Based on routines and turn-taking[cite: 281].
5 Constructivism (Vygotsky)
[cite_start]

Central Idea: Language is constructed via social guidance within the Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD)[cite: 291].

    [cite_start]
  • Scaffolding (help from a guide) is key[cite: 294].
    • [cite_start]
  • Language begins as a social tool, then becomes internal thought[cite: 293].

1.2 Stages of First Language Acquisition

All children typically pass through these physiological and motor stages:

1. Prelinguistic Stage (Babbling)
Approx. 0 - 12 months

Before words. [cite_start]Starts with cooing ("ooo") and moves to babbling ("bababa" or "baga")[cite: 323, 328].

[cite_start]

Key Feature: No real meaning yet, but practices rhythm and intonation[cite: 331].

üë∂ Image: Baby making sounds with comic bubble "ba-ba-ba".
2. One-word Stage (Holophrastic)
Approx. 12 - 18 months
[cite_start]

The child uses a single word to express a complete sentence (e.g., "Milk" = "I want milk")[cite: 336, 340].

[cite_start]

Phenomena: Overextension (calling all animals "dog") and Underextension[cite: 341, 342].

ü•õ Image: Child pointing at milk saying just "MILK".
3. Two-word Stage
Approx. 18 - 24 months
[cite_start]

Combinations of two words with clear grammatical relationships (Agent + Action like "Mommy go")[cite: 345, 348].

[cite_start]

Key Feature: This is the real beginning of syntax[cite: 353].

4. Complex Structures Stage
24+ months (2-4 years)

Rapid expansion. [cite_start]Use of questions ("Why he sad?"), negations ("I no want"), and morphology (past tense -ed)[cite: 357, 366].

[cite_start]

Common Error: Overgeneralization (e.g., saying "goed" instead of "went")[cite: 369].
