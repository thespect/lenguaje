Theories, Stages, and The Role of Environment

1.1 Five Theories of First Language Acquisition (L1)

1. Behaviorism (B. F. Skinner)

Language is learned by imitating, repeating, and receiving reinforcement (Stimulus ‚Üí Response ‚Üí Reinforcement). [cite_start]A child says a word, gets a smile (reward), and repeats it [cite: 240-248].

Criticism: Cannot explain creativity (new sentences).

2. Innate Language Acquisition (Noam Chomsky)

Humans are born with a LAD (Language Acquisition Device) and Universal Grammar. [cite_start]Input only activates this biological capacity, explaining why children learn even with "poverty of stimulus" [cite: 254-260].

3. Cognitivism (Jean Piaget)

Language depends on cognitive development. [cite_start]A child must understand the concept of "time" before using the past tense [cite: 266-273].

4. Interactionism (Bruner / Tomasello)

Language is acquired through social interaction. [cite_start]Caregivers use Motherese (LASS) to scaffold learning through routines and turn-taking [cite: 276-281].

5. Constructivism (Lev Vygotsky)

Language is constructed within the ZPD (Zone of Proximal Development). [cite_start]Social interaction with a guide leads to internal thought [cite: 290-296].

1.2 Stages of Acquisition

1. Prelinguistic (0-12 months)

Babbling: Reduplicated ("bababa") or variegated ("baga"). [cite_start]No meaning yet, but practices rhythm [cite: 322-331].

2. One-word / Holophrastic (12-18 months)

A single word expresses a complete idea. E.g., "Milk" = "I want milk". [cite_start]Overextension occurs (calling all animals "dog") [cite: 334-341].

3. Two-word Stage (18-24 months)

Beginning of syntax. Agent + Action ("Mommy go"). [cite_start]No function words [cite: 343-353].

4. Complex Structures (24+ months)

Questions, negations, and morphology (-ed, -s). [cite_start]Overgeneralization is common (e.g., "I goed") [cite: 354-369].

1.3 The Role of the Environment

The family is the primary linguistic environment. Key factors include:

  • Quantity of Input: More conversation = larger vocabulary.
  • Quality of Input: Reading aloud and varied words stimulate development.

  • Affective Environment: Security allows children to take risks [cite: 404-415].
