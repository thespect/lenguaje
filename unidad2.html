2.1 Simultaneous Acquisition

1. What does it mean?

[cite_start]

It is when a child learns two languages at the same time from birth, usually because both languages are used at home or in their everyday environment [cite: 222-223].

2. How learning occurs

  • The child receives constant input in both languages from infancy.
  • They learn each language separately, not as if they were mixed together.
    • [cite_start]
  • They go through the same stages as a monolingual child (babbling, one word, two words, etc.), only in two languages [cite: 224-227].

3. Cognitive advantages

  • Greater mental flexibility.
  • Better attention span and ability to switch between tasks.
    • [cite_start]
  • Early development of metalinguistic awareness (they realize that language is a system) [cite: 228-231].

4. Normal characteristics

  • They may mix words from both languages (code-switching), but this is completely normal and shows that the child is using all their resources to communicate.
    • [cite_start]
  • Vocabularies may seem smaller in each language, but the total combined vocabulary is usually equal or greater [cite: 232-234].

5. Important factors

  • Consistency in exposure (both languages should be heard regularly).
  • Meaningful interactions with speakers of both languages.
    • [cite_start]
  • Positive reinforcement, reading, and routines in both languages [cite: 235-238].

2.2 Sequential Acquisition

1. What does it mean?

[cite_start]

It is when a person begins to learn a second language after having established their first language (usually after age 3, although it can be later) [cite: 242-243].

2. How learning occurs

  • Learning is no longer entirely natural as in L1; it combines exposure, instruction, memory, and conscious practice.
  • The child uses L1 as a basis for understanding L2 (mentally translating, comparing rules).
    • [cite_start]
  • Learning can take place at school, in the community, in bilingual programs, or out of social necessity [cite: 244-247].

3. Common stages in L2 learning

  1. Silent Period: The learner listens a lot but speaks little; they try to understand first.
  2. Early Production: Short sentences, single words (e.g., ‚Äúyes,‚Äù ‚Äúno,‚Äù ‚Äúteacher‚Äù).
  3. Emergent Speech: Simple sentences; mixing with L1.
    4. [cite_start]
  4. Intermediate & Advanced Production: Greater vocabulary, more complex structures, more fluent communication [cite: 248-256].

4. Influencing factors

  • Age: The younger the learner, the easier it is to achieve natural pronunciation; older learners acquire academic vocabulary more quickly.
  • Quantity and quality of input: More exposure = more progress.
  • Motivation: Need, interest, personal goals.
  • Attitude toward the language/culture: Acceptance facilitates learning; rejection or fear slows it down.
    • [cite_start]
  • School and family support: Modeling, reading, guided practice [cite: 257-263].

5. Typical characteristics

  • Transfer or interference: The learner uses rules from L1 in L2 (e.g., ‚ÄúShe have‚Äù).
  • Code-switching: Mixing languages when speaking, which is normal.
    • [cite_start]
  • Strategic learning: The student uses memory, repetition, rules, conscious practice [cite: 264-268].

6. Advantages of sequential learning

  • Improved memory, concentration, and problem solving.
  • Broader cultural competencies.
  • Better academic performance in areas such as reading and linguistic analysis.
    • [cite_start]
  • Development of metalinguistic skills (understanding how language works) [cite: 269-273].
